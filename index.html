About

I’m so happy. This new release is the first in a long time that I’ve actually been truly happy with—no pretending. It’s absolutely fantastic. Seriously, that’s one of the best feelings an artist can have: creating something I’m genuinely proud of, without reservations. It’s easy to get caught up in technicalities—distribution, marketing, the "right" way—but at the core, it’s about the music itself. Reaching this point of genuine satisfaction is a huge accomplishment. That authenticity will shine through to listeners, and it’s a powerful driver for everything moving forward.

I’ll keep that feeling close. Being an open-source artist helps. It’s funny—people don’t know how to parse it because the term isn’t official in music. That’s problematic when others struggle with uncommon ground. But that’s where I thrive. It’s fascinating: the power of a unique, uncodified term versus the industry’s preference for categories. The "problem" of uncommon ground is real. People try to fit me into genres, business models, or roles—but "open source artist" doesn’t slot in. It causes confusion ("What does this mean? Can I use your music?"), skepticism ("Is this a gimmick?"), and difficulty for gatekeepers. But that’s precisely where my strength lies. It gives me unique identity, authenticity, and new avenues for engagement—remixes, collaboration, community-driven support. I’m defining a new way of operating, free from constraints.

I’m building a new path. Heck yeah! I’m owning this. I’m not just making music; I’m redefining how to interact with it, share it, and build community. That’s powerful. But the world isn’t ready—and is ready—simultaneously. There are always two sides to this coin. Traditional structures don’t recognize "open source artist." It confuses them, demands education, and lacks conventional pathways. Yet for digitally native audiences—those valuing collaboration, transparency, and freedom—it resonates deeply. I’m challenging the system itself. It takes clarity and confidence to stand in this uncommon ground, and I’ve got it. But it’s not about me. It’s about the music and the concepts enabling us to keep making good music. Open-sourcing conflicts with artists relying on music for survival. I believe music should have no relationship to money to remain untainted. They say, "You need to survive." I agree—but I deny that music must be your sole backbone for survival. Money will come.

Music suffers profoundly from monetary incentive: creative compromise, loss of authenticity, market-driven stress. I also deny that without monetization, artists must reduce output or abandon music. My methods prove otherwise: the 1:1 pomodoro workflow (25m on/25m off—yin/yang balance, not 5:1), producing without listening to subs or headphones (reducing ear fatigue). After 10+ years of producing, amplified by psychedelic insights, I know these approaches will become standard for fresh music. Current music is stale because of financial pressure and fatiguing practices. Cut the incentive, cut the fatigue, and watch old systems crumble.

We’ll create ten-fold what we desire—without bloat, pretense, or burnout. This is a revolution: severing money’s corrupting link to creation, eliminating unnecessary fatigue, and embracing intuitive workflows. It’s about artist well-being, artistic integrity, and creative innovation. I’m not just an "open-source artist" in distribution—but in process, philosophy, and purpose. That’s why this release feels right. I’m creating from a place of radical authenticity, and it's working!

Yours truly,
